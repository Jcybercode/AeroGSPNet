Figure 1: AeroGSPNet Model Architecture

AeroGSPNet redesigns a hierarchical modeling and abstraction strategy to simultaneously improve the accuracy and efficiency of aerodynamic prediction. Specifically, the model first employs Graph Feature Embedding to reduce the number of points involved in global modeling from N to N‚Ä≤, and then utilizes a Patch Attention module to adaptively aggregate points into G ‚â™ N local patches, such that global dependencies are modeled exclusively at the patch level. This results in a hierarchical abstraction process of N ‚Üí N‚Ä≤ ‚Üí G, enabling the training of drag prediction tasks on a single RTX 4090 GPU.

More Visualized Error Results

Based on a quantitative analysis of the error distribution, we selected the ten samples with the largest prediction errors for in-depth investigation. The results indicate that the errors are predominantly concentrated in the following regions:

  1. Frontal intake grille and bumper area, where the presence of a high-pressure stagnation point leads to sharp physical gradients, resulting in deviations in capturing the pressure core;
  2. Wheels, rims, and wheel-arch regions, which involve coupled rotational motion and complex cavity‚Äìflow interactions;
  3. Rear end and rear bumper area, where flow separation from the vehicle body gives rise to a low-pressure recirculation wake, and vortex shedding induces pronounced fluctuations in base-pressure prediction.

Error Analysis in High-Curvature Regions

Error Analysis in High-Curvature Regions: The visualization consists of two parts: the top row highlights regions of the vehicle surface with high geometric curvature, while the bottom row displays the corresponding absolute error distributions in those areas. The comparison reveals that prediction errors are primarily concentrated in localized regions with complex geometric topologies, specifically the inner edges of the wheel arches, the fine details of the front grille, the junctions between the side mirrors and the body, and the sharp ridges of the rear diffuser. These results indicate that errors are highly localized and predominantly occur at geometric singularities with extremely small curvature radii, whereas errors on smooth, flat surfaces remain negligible.
